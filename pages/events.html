Events, for iMessage

Find mutually available times and create events all from iMessage.

Problem

How do friends communicate?

Group messaging. You have the freedom to communicate what you want, when you want, and to multiple people at once. Group messages, however, are an archive of thoughts, and are not optimized for finding a mutual time to get together.

Solution

Events for iMessage allows friends to find mutually available times and organize integrated events all from iMessage.


Start the conversation.

Checking availability and planning events has always been a conversation. From the simple "I miss you, let's hangout" text to sharing availabilities and creating a Calendar linked event, Events for iMessage makes this conversation digital.

Make it an event.

Events for iMessage brings both casual and professional event planning directly to iMessage. Want to set up a mutually available interview time? Meet up with old friends for dinner? Suggest events as an organizer and send your own availability as an attendee. Share the event to Calendar with just a few swipes.

Process

Creating an app that lives within the context of a Messages conversation introduces unique design constraints. I had to work around the lack of screen real estate and think about the existing visual elements that users expect to see within iMessage. iMessage apps are typically used in the context of fast-paced, informal conversations. As such, keeping the number of interaction steps as low as possible ensures that the organic flow of messaging is not lost.

The first challenge was creating a simple, minimal interface for the calendar’s integration into the app. If we’re trying to figure out when people are available, we must look at hours, days, weeks, and maybe even the month to get an accurate picture of one’s schedule. I decided to use a “painting” touch gesture so that users can easily “paint” their availability on the calendar. This is a visual representation of the organic, fluid flow that is necessary for an unobtrusive iMessage app. Also, people may be familiar with this gesture from the most popular scheduling apps, which makes it simpler for users to feel comfortable with the app’s flow. Though it is sometime necessary to create custom controls, using a pre-established interaction is favorable in this case as the unique value in this app does not lie in the calendar by itself.

  • I used a step-by-step flow for the availability and event creation in order to keep things simple.
  • Will the inviter’s view look different than the invitee’s view? (only where necessary).
  • How should people’s responses be visually differentiated? (Stacked opacities of different colors).
  • Should people “paint” when they are available, or when they are busy? (Available).
  • Should we display the hours before or after the event is created? (Before, as the time of day is important for any get together).
  • Will overlaying existing calendar events (iCal) feel out of place for a casual, friends-based event app? (No, it’s important to see all obligations to have an accurate schedule).

Once the “inviter” sends his/her availability, the event is created. Now, all participants have the option of adding integrations to the event. Though I experimented with multiple 3rd party integrations that make sense for events, I narrowed it down to the following 4 for simplicity and clarity:

  1. Photos: Create and be automatically subscribed to a shared album with iCloud Photo Sharing.
  2. Maps: Suggest and vote for the eventʼs location with Maps.
  3. Music: Add a shared Apple Music playlist through MusicKit with your friendsʼ preferences in the mix.
  4. Pay: Send or receive money from individuals through Apple Pay in iOS 11.
